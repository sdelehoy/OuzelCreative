The Ultimate Post-Event Guide to Turning Connections into Opportunities

February 18, 2026

What to do After Networking Events: How to Followup, Reward Yourself for Success, and Your Post-Event Action Plan

You crushed it at your last event. You met amazing people, had great conversations, and even exchanged a few business cards. Or maybe the crowd was quiet, you didn’t click with anyone, or you spent most of the night hiding near the snack table. Now what?

What happens after the event is what sets you up for success. Too many people let their new connections fade into the abyss of forgotten LinkedIn requests or let their doubts spiral them into giving up. Don’t be that person.

In this post, we'll cover:

  1. Why you should reward yourself after networking (and how it helps you grow).
  2. How to follow up without being annoying (because nobody likes a pushy networker).
  3. What do do if the event didn’t go as planned.

Let’s dive in!

Part 1: What to do After Networking Events: Your Post-Event Action Plan

Networking can be exhausting, especially if you’re an introvert. But here’s the thing: You did it! No matter what happened, you showed up! That’s a win. And that deserves recognition.

Why Rewarding Yourself Matters

1. Enhances Motivation

Celebrating small wins trains your brain to associate networking with positive feelings and trains your brain to see networking as worth it. If you met your goal of meeting three new people, treat yourself to something small to celebrate.

2. It Resets your Mindset

A little self-kindness prevents you from giving up. If you didn’t meet your goal, congratulate yourself on the try– getting out there is what counts.

3. Reduces Post-Event Stress

A little self-praise eases post-networking anxiety and makes future events feel less intimidating. Beating yourself up increases anxiety for next time. Give yourself a compliment: ”I handled that conversation like a pro—next time will be even easier!”

4. Reinforces Growth

Recognizing your progress builds confidence and keeps you improving. If the first time you struggled to start conversations and the next time you initiated three, focus on how you just leveled up!

5. Incentivizes Productivity

Rewards keep you motivated to keep networking. Find small rewards that help you feel excited to keep taking steps towards your goal and larger rewards that keep you focused on the big picture.

How to Reward Yourself (Even if it felt like a bust)

  • Small wins: A coffee, a walk in nature, or a quick dance break.
  • Big wins: A fun outing, a new book, or a relaxing bath.
  • Non-material rewards: Write down what went well and tell someone about it.

Pro tip: Make your reward immediate (within 24 hours) for maximum motivation. Focus on progress, not perfection. Every event is practice.

Part 2: How to Follow Up (Without Being Annoying)

Following up is key to turning connections into real relationships. But nobody likes a spammy networker. Here’s how to do it right.

Step 1: Organize Your Contacts

Use a spreadsheet or app (like LinkedIn or Google Contacts) to track who you met. Note key details (e.g., "Loves urban farming, mentioned needing a composting partner”).

Step 2: Personalize Your Message

  • Bad follow-up: ”Hi, nice to meet you! Let’s connect!”
  • Good follow-up: "Hey [Name], it was great chatting about urban farming at [Name Event]! I loved hearing about your composting project—would love to stay in touch."

Step 3: Keep It Short & Sweet

1-3 sentences max.

Example: "Hey [Name], enjoyed our chat about zero-waste tips! Here’s that article I mentioned: [link]. Let’s grab coffee soon!”

Step 4: Offer Value (Without Asking for Anything)

  • Share a resource (e.g., "I found this great podcast on sustainable fashion—thought you’d like it!”).
  • Make an introduction (e.g., "I know someone working on a similar project—should I connect you?”).

Step 5: Follow Up Again (If Needed)

If they don’t respond, send one polite follow-up after a week. Then move on.

Final Thought: Networking Is a Marathon, Not a Sprint

Networking is a skill. The difference between success and failure? Persistence. If the event was a flop: Reflect, adjust, try again. If you’re feeling discouraged: Reward yourself and keep going. If you’re not sure what to do next. Start small and build confidence. The real power of networking happens after the event. By following up thoughtfully, you’ll turn fleeting connections into lasting relationships.
